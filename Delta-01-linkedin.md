## THE POST (copy from here)

Forward Deployed Engineer job posts grew 1,165% last year.

The role everyone calls "just sales" pays a $174K median base — with zero commission.

Here's the job quietly eating both "engineer" and "consultant," and why every AI lab is now fighting to hire it 👇

Start with the data. Bloomberry analyzed 1,000 FDE job posts:

• $173,816 median base. 70% include equity. 0% carry a sales quota.
• Python in 66%. AI agents in 35%.
• 45% were software engineers first.

These are engineers who happen to sit with customers — not salespeople who happen to code.

Now the history most people miss.

Palantir invented this role almost two decades ago for a brutal reason: their customers were intelligence agencies who couldn't tell you what they needed. You can't email a requirements doc to the CIA.

So instead of dragging the problem back to HQ, Palantir sent engineers forward — onto the customer's site, into the room where the work happened. Internally, they called them "Deltas."

Until 2016, Palantir had more Deltas than product engineers (per Nabeel Qureshi, 8-year alum).

That bet built a company running 80% gross margins in 2023 — while Accenture, the gold standard of real services, runs 32%. Palantir looked like a consulting shop and earned like software.

The idea that made it work came from a strange place: a French restaurant.

Alex Karp reportedly noticed that in a great French restaurant, the waitstaff isn't bolted onto the kitchen — they're part of it. They taste the dish, read the table, walk back, and the chef changes the duck. Delivery and product, one loop.

That loop is the entire job: go into the field, build the hacky thing that works, feed what you learn back into the product.

Now the present:

• 22 of OpenAI's 311 open roles are forward-deployed / solutions roles (a16z, 2025).
• Sequoia's math: for every $1 spent on software, $6 is spent on services. AI finally lets you capture the $6.
• MIT: 95% of enterprise AI pilots show no measurable impact.

The model was never the bottleneck. The last mile is.

Before the skeptics arrive — "isn't this just a consultant in a hoodie?":

A consultant hands you a deck. A Delta hands you production code, owns the outcome, and is still on-site at 2am when it breaks. No quota. No deck. Just the result.

And here's the part nobody says out loud: the AI labs are hiring armies of FDEs to build the very tools — agents, playbooks, MCP servers — that may one day do the FDE's job. The role might be training the machine to replace it.

Or it becomes the most durable job in tech. Nobody knows yet. That's what makes it the most interesting seat in AI.

I'm writing a series on it — Forward Deployed Engineering, done right. It's called Delta.

FDEs: what's the part of this job nobody warned you about?
