Everyone's panicking that AI makes us dumber. I just learned a stack I'd never touched — wide AND deep enough to ship — in about 48 hours. Here's the mental model a 12-year-old can run.

Old rule: you could be wide OR deep. Pick one. It took years.

Picture a mountain range.

The MAP is width — the whole territory from above, every peak and the trails between them. You can't climb a map, but without one you don't know which mountain matters.

A LADDER is depth — pick one spot and go down until you can actually operate there.

The GATE is proof — a turnstile that opens only if what you built actually works.

AI ends the old trade-off: it draws the whole map in an afternoon and builds a ladder anywhere you point. That's the 10×.

The trap: AI writes such fluent explanations that you feel the warm click of "I get it" — and got nothing. Reading a manual about swimming is not swimming. You'll still drown, now with excellent theoretical posture.

So define "better" honestly: not "I know more facts." Better = you can deliver a verified result. A receipt, not a feeling. That's the Gate.

Map → Ladder → Gate → Teach.

What field would you map this weekend if drawing the map took an afternoon, not a year?
